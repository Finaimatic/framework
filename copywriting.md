# Part 1 — Foundations

## Core Philosophy

Make the reader think: **"There's no way this email was for anyone else but me."**

Three things make a cold email not boring:
1. **Personal** — feels written for that one person
2. **Short** — gets the point across in as few lines as possible
3. **Valuable** — gives something worth their time

What makes cold emails boring:
- Impersonal or irrelevant — "industry leaders like yourself" sent to 10,000 people
- Unnecessarily long — nobody reads essays from strangers
- Generic value props — "optimize," "streamline," "save time"

## Josh Braun Craft Principles

1. **Write with an eraser** — Remove every unnecessary word
2. **Be cheeky** — Personality matters. Make them smile. Their brain says "that made me feel good, I'm going to respond."
3. **Be specific** — Credibility comes through precision. "10-12% MoM boost" beats "significant results."
4. **Don't say "we reduce costs"** — Say "e-commerce companies in the pet space overpaying for shipping by 10-15%"
5. **Use loss aversion** — Frame around what they're losing by NOT acting
6. **Don't make the offer too good to be true** — Plausible beats grandiose

## The Rules

1. Under 100 words (ideally 60–90)
2. More about them than you
3. Benefits over features
4. Solve a problem they actually have
5. Personalize or make it hyper-relevant
6. Pick your offer wisely
7. Provide value before asking for anything

**Hard rules (never break):**
- No em dashes in copy — use periods or commas instead
- No "just following up," "one more thought," "quick check-in"
- Every follow-up must stand alone — no references to previous emails
- Plain text only — no HTML, no images
- One CTA per email

## Language Constraints (What Never to Write)

**Spam words to avoid:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Urgency | "Act now," "Limited time," "Urgent," "Don't miss out," "Expires soon" |
| Money | "Free," "Discount," "Cash," "Save big," "Earn $," "100% free" |
| Too good to be true | "Guarantee," "No obligation," "Risk-free," "Winner," "Congratulations" |
| Salesy | "Buy now," "Order today," "Click here," "Call now," "Subscribe" |
| Suspicious | "This isn't spam," "You've been selected," "Dear friend" |

**Generic B2B words to cut entirely:**
"Optimize," "streamline," "leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "save time," "save money"

# Part 2 — Anatomy of One Email

Five components. Each section below contains everything for that component in one place.

## 1. Observation (Opener / First Line)

What you noticed about them. Must be relevant to the problem you solve.

**Trigger-based openers:**
- Hiring for a specific role
- Recent funding
- New C-level hire
- Product launch
- Merger / acquisition
- Increase in ad spend
- Bad Glassdoor reviews (if selling HR tools)
- Short average tenure (if selling retention tools)

**Example openers:**
- "Noticed you have over 50 open positions on your job portal"
- "Saw that your latest blog post doesn't include a Meta tag"
- "Congrats on crossing the 5-year mark"

### 3 First-Line Opener Types

**Type 1: Observation (General Triggers)** — Call out something without over-explaining.
```
Hey John, saw you offer a free audit on your site.
```

**Type 2: Pain (Specific Triggers)** — Connect the trigger directly to the "why."
```
Hey John, here is a top of funnel outbound playbook. With {{company}} hiring for a BDR, I assume generating top of funnel is a priority.
```

**Type 3: Industry (Enterprise / Traditional)** — Hot topic they're aware of or impacted by.
```
Subject: tariffs
Hey John, with costs rising on imports from China, are you considering your options?
```

## 2. Value Proposition

State in terms of outcomes, not features:
- **Make money** — increase revenue, pipeline, conversions
- **Save money** — reduce costs, prevent waste
- **Save time** — automate, simplify, eliminate manual work

Bad: "We help companies optimize their sales process"
Good: "We help tech scale-ups reduce new employee churn from 30% to 10% or less"

### 3 Value Proposition Styles

**Style 1: Show the Cost of the Problem** — Observation/pain → here's what that's actually costing you → specific number

> Hey John, most businesses at the 5-year mark are leaving $50K–$200K in available capital on the table — typically because they don't know what they qualify for.
>
> Based on your revenue range, {{company}} likely qualifies for more than you'd expect.
>
> Would you be open to hearing more?

**Style 2: Peer Proof** — Observation/pain → other companies like you are doing X → result they're getting

> Hey John, congrats on crossing the 5-year mark. A lot of businesses at your stage are using growth capital to fund expansion without touching their cash reserves.
>
> One owner in {{industry}} locked in $150K at terms that let him keep full ownership.
>
> Would something like that be useful for {{company}}?

**Style 3: Specific Outcome** — Observation/pain → here's the specific outcome → want to see if it applies?

> Hey John, since you just opened a second location, I'd imagine managing cash flow across both is a priority.
>
> We recently helped a {{industry}} business secure $200K in growth capital in under 2 weeks with no equity or collateral.
>
> Would it make sense to see what {{company}} qualifies for?

## 3. Social Proof

- Use numbers (%, $, time saved)
- Name real companies if recognizable
- One sentence max
- "We recently helped {{customer}} achieve {{result}} in {{timeframe}}"

## 4. Call to Action

Always end with a question. Never "Let me know" (too vague).

**Soft (performs better):**
- "Worth exploring?"
- "Can I share how?"
- "Sound relevant?"
- "Worth a quick look?"

**Hard (direct ask):**
- "Worth a chat?"
- "Open for a quick call this week?"

## 5. P.S. Line (optional)

- Second social proof point
- Personalized observation
- Routing ask: "P.S. Let me know if someone else would be the right person to talk to about this"

## Persona Overlay: ATL vs BTL

Persona changes how each component above is written.

### Above-the-Line (ATL) — VPs, C-Level, Directors

**Mindset:** Strategic. Think past and future. Want validation, not problems solved.

**Length:** 2–3 sentences only

**Language:** "Revenue impact," "competitive advantage," "board-level priority," "risk mitigation"

**What they care about:** Revenue, risk reduction, competitive positioning, board/investor optics

**Fatal mistake:** Mentioning operational problems = immediate delegation to a subordinate. Your thread dies.

**When to target ATL:** Deal size >$50K ACV, strategic purchase, requires budget approval from top

**Example:**
```
Subject: Q1 revenue efficiency

{{firstName}},

If you could identify revenue leakage 3 months faster, what would that mean for next year's targets?

We helped {{similar_company}}'s CEO do exactly that.

15 minutes to explore?
```

### Below-the-Line (BTL) — Managers, Individual Contributors, End-Users

**Mindset:** Tactical. Think present. Want problems solved now. Care about daily workflow.

**Length:** 3–4 sentences acceptable

**Language:** "Stop spending 3 hours on...," "Eliminate the manual work of...," "Save X hours/week"

**What they care about:** Daily pain points, time savings, looking good to their boss

**Fatal mistake:** Talking ROI and strategic impact = too abstract, does not resonate

**When to target BTL:** Product-led growth, bottom-up sale, need an internal champion first

**Example:**
```
Subject: 3 hours back

Hey {{firstName}},

Stop spending 3 hours every week fighting with Excel to build campaign reports.

We helped {{similar_company}}'s ops team cut their reporting time by 70%.

Want to see how?
```

### Who to Target

| Situation | Target |
|---|---|
| Deal size >$50K ACV | ATL |
| Strategic / transformation purchase | ATL |
| Product-led growth | BTL |
| Bottom-up sale | BTL |
| Complex enterprise sale | Both (multi-thread) |

# Part 3 — Pattern Library

Two ways to assemble the Part 2 components into a finished email. The **Named Frameworks** are reusable patterns with full examples. The **Structural Variations** are skeletal scaffolds for a first email — most are a stripped-down form of one of the frameworks. Pick whichever entry point fits how you think.

## Named Frameworks

| Framework | Best For | Creator |
|---|---|---|
| Do the Math | Quantifiable ROI | Thibaut Souyris |
| Short Trigger | Quick, under-30-word emails | Guillermo Blanco |
| Challenge of Similar Companies | Industry-wide pain points | Patrick Trümpi |
| Neutral Insight | Sharing third-party resources | Chelsea Castle |
| Leader Responsibilities | Role-specific curiosity | Vin Matano |
| Ask Before Pitch / Pattern Interrupt | Flipping the script | Will Allred |
| Not Too Different Persona | Peer similarity | BowTiedSalesGuy |
| Upfront Value | Leading with free value | Jordan Crawford |
| Leverage Content in Outbound | Lead magnets as entry point | Ethan Parker |
| "Why Are You Paying?" | Analogy-based persuasion | Leif Bisping |
| Write a Good Cold Email | Problem-first structure | Josh Braun |
| HOT Outreach | Peak interest → value → CTA | — |
| Manual Email Style | Casual, human, low-pressure | Josh Braun |

### Do the Math
Trigger → quick pitch → calculation → CTA

> Mary, noticed you have over 50 open positions on your job portal.
>
> We help tech scale-ups reduce their new employee churn from 30% to 10% or less.
>
> With a typical cost of mis-hire around $30,000 per employee, this would mean going from 15 mis-hires to 5, resulting in $300,000 saved.
>
> Worth a chat?

### Short Trigger-Based Outreach
Under 30 words. Relevant trigger → validation + value → CTA.

> G, saw that Reach's latest blog post doesn't include a Meta tag which can affect its online visibility.
>
> {{client}} improved theirs by creating meta tags using ChatGPT.
>
> Can I share how?

### Challenge of Similar Companies
Industry pain you've seen across multiple accounts.

> Hey Patrick,
>
> We are working with a lot of security officers at banks who faced losses of $9K per hour due to ransomware and had phishing victims every 11 seconds.
>
> We have a global team of experts available 24/7 to react in hours not days. When is the last time you tested your plan?
>
> P.S. I would attach a link but we both work in security 😅

### Neutral Insight
Third-party resource → tie to their context → soft pitch.

> George, do you read Outreach's blog?
>
> Given you're likely ramping reps, I thought you'd find it interesting.
>
> The VP of Sales Dev at Segment wrote about how she scaled her team to a $3.2B acquisition — they did it without using canned templates.
>
> Check it out. P.S. Any thoughts on my last note?

### Leader Responsibilities
Role-specific question → curiosity → personalized note.

> Armand, as a Sales leader, curious how your reps prioritize which accounts to reach out to?
>
> If I can create a list for your team of accounts researching competitors like Salesforce, would it be worth a conversation?
>
> Either way, congrats on recently being named Top Sales Coach!

### Ask Before Pitch / Pattern Interrupt
Open-ended question → trigger → how product solves → PS relevance.

> Hey Will, think this would help George & Anne?
>
> You're hiring new sellers. Despite the same templates, some reps get results and others don't.
>
> Lavender gives you a clearer picture on why things work — and identifies personalization plays for the team.
>
> Let me know.

### Not Too Different Persona
Position as someone who already knows their world.

> I work with [ICP similar to target]. They tell me they struggle with [problem]...

### Upfront Value
Give something useful before asking anything.

> Here's a list of every founder at every company that just implemented HubSpot... I hope it was helpful.

### Leverage Content in Outbound
Lead magnet as entry point → PS explains relevance.

> Hey Jason — we've compiled a cheat sheet on how folks like [social proof] are getting their AEs self-sourcing more than 30% of their own pipeline.
>
> Can I send it over to you?
>
> P.S. Relevant because I saw you recently decreased your SDR headcount and are hiring 3 more AEs.

### "Why Are You Paying?"
Life situation analogy → obvious choice → compare to prospect's current solution.

Set the stage with a familiar situation. Describe the obvious choice. Then compare it to what the prospect is currently doing — and make them realize they're overpaying or overworking for something that should be simple.

### Write a Good Cold Email (Josh Braun)
Think through before writing:
1. Who are you targeting?
2. What problem are you trying to solve?
3. What are they currently doing to solve it?
4. What's wrong with their current solution?
5. How does your solution solve it differently?

Focus on what sucks about how they currently get the job done — not your value prop. Solutions have no value without a problem people can relate to.

> James, honestly now — did you spend your youth dreaming about someday hard-pasting entire Excel pages into Google Sheets and manually making adjustments, one sheet at a time, to determine payouts?

### HOT Outreach
**Section 1 — Peak interest (the problem you found):**
- Say the problem directly: "Your website is slow," "Your podcast clips are edited poorly"
- Mention something not obvious: "You have a TikTok account but no TikTok shop"
- Shared connection: "I also love X"

**Section 2 — The value (what makes you an expert):**
Give them the answer. Whatever you want them to pay you for, give some of it right now.
- "If you change your email to long form, you will easily get a 20% open rate difference"
- "I re-edited a video of yours — it's here and why I did XYZ"

**Section 3 — About you and CTA:**
- I am X from Y. I have done Z. I think I can help.
- Always end with a question. Never "Let me know." Never "Let's get a call."

### Manual Email Style (Josh Braun)
Casual. Human. Low-pressure.

> Hey Josh — Subscribed to your email list a few weeks ago. I've been doing digital marketing for eight years — have a few unconventional ideas that might goose sales of your Badass Guide without offering discounts.
>
> I don't get paid unless you make more.
>
> Worth batting around a few ideas? Henry.
>
> P.S. Several course creators I'm working with are seeing a 10-12% MoM boost in sales.

Why it works: personalization + lowers resistance ("don't get paid unless you make more") + plausible social proof + curiosity + peer tone.

# Part 4 — The Sequence

## Cadence Overview

| Email | Timing | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Email 1 | Day 0 | Get the reply — 80% of positive replies happen here |
| Email 2 | Day 3 (same thread) | Add value, different angle |
| Email 3 | Day 17 (new subject) | Final attempt, very soft CTA |

**Value prop rotation:**
- Email 1: Save money
- Email 2: Make money
- Email 3: Save time

Always add the "So What": "Save 3 hours/month so your SDRs spend more time actually selling" — not just "save 3 hours/month."

## Email 1 (Day 0)

Goal: get the reply. This is where 80% of positive replies happen. Assemble it using any framework or skeleton from **Part 3 — Pattern Library**, and rotate to the "save money" value prop.

## Email 2 — Follow-Up (Day 3, Same Thread)

- Reply to your own email (same thread, no new subject)
- Lead with value: lead magnet, resource, case study
- Shorter than Email 1
- Different value prop angle — never repeat Email 1's pitch

```
{{firstName}}, here's [the lead magnet / resource]

[Social proof]

[Value prop restated differently]

[CTA]
```

**What works for breakup-style follow-ups:**
- "Am I reaching the right person?"
- "Is there someone else who handles this?"

**What doesn't work:**
- Guilt trips, begging, "you must be getting chased by an alligator" humor

## Email 3 — Final Touch (Day 17, New Subject Line)

- New subject line (fresh thread)
- Use a different Email 1 variation than your first send
- Very soft CTA — aim for any kind of answer
- If no reply → wait 3 months, then re-engage

```
Subject: [New subject line]

[New opener — different angle from Email 1]

[Very soft CTA]

"If this isn't you, who should I be talking to about {{topic}}?"
```

## Re-Engagement (Weeks/Months Later)

For leads who went cold, closed-lost deals, or prospects who ghosted.

**Key principles:**
- Acknowledge the gap — don't pretend prior contact didn't happen
- Lead with what changed — new feature, case study, market shift
- No-oriented questions get more replies ("Would it be crazy to reconnect?" beats "Want to chat?")
- Very soft CTAs: "Still relevant?" or "Worth another look?"

### No-Oriented Question (Rebook)
```
Subject: quick question

{{firstName}},

Would it be crazy to reconnect?

I know we talked {{timeframe}} ago about {{topic}}.

We've since [new development] which directly addresses the [objection] concern.

Still relevant?
```

### Closed-Lost Reactivation
```
Subject: since we last talked

{{firstName}},

When we talked in {{month}}, you mentioned {{objection}} was the blocker.

Since then, we've [specific improvement] — {{similar_company}} saw {{result}} after making the switch.

Worth another look?
```

### Ramp Re-Engagement (New Angle)
```
Subject: different angle

{{firstName}},

Reached out a while back about {{original_topic}} — different reason this time.

Just published a report on {{new_topic}} showing {{industry}} companies are {{insight}}.

Thought of {{company}} given {{trigger}}.

If this isn't you, who handles {{topic}} on your team?
```

## Subject Lines

**Rules:**
- 2–4 words ideal — shorter outperforms
- All lowercase — feels personal, not promotional
- No spam trigger words ("free," "guarantee," "act now")
- No exclamation marks, no ALL CAPS
- Relevant to the body — deceptive subjects destroy trust
- Follow-up Email 2: RE: (same subject). Email 3: new subject.

**Formulas:**

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Personalized | `{{trigger_topic}}`, `{{metric}} at {{company}}`, `{{years}} years` |
| Curiosity | `quick question`, `one thing about {{topic}}`, `{{company}} → {{outcome}}` |
| Direct value | `3 hours back`, `{{number}} potential leads`, `Q1 revenue efficiency` |
| Two-word formula | Exactly 2 words, lowercase, no sales words: "marketing data," "hiring speed," "pipeline math" |

**A/B testing:**
- Test 2 subject lines at a time
- Minimum 100 sends per variant
- Measure reply rate, not just open rate — high opens with low replies = clickbait

**Top performer benchmarks:**

| Tier | Avg Subject Length |
|---|---|
| Top performers | 42 chars |
| Average | 47 chars |
| Poor | 54 chars |

# Part 5 — Personalization

## 6 Data Buckets (Ranked by Value)

1. **Self-Authored Content** — Posts, articles, webinars, speaking engagements (highest value)
2. **Engaged Content** — What they commented on, shared, liked
3. **Self-Identified Traits** — LinkedIn headline, about section, company description
4. **Junk Drawer** — Personal interests, volunteer work, languages, schools (use sparingly)
5. **Background Centric** — Tenure, career trajectory, mutual connections, certifications
6. **Company Level** — Funding, hiring, product launches, news, M&A (24 data points)

## Hook Types

**Strong Hook (Verbatim Tie):** Direct quote or reference from their content.
"In your recent post about X, you mentioned Y..."

**Lite Hook (Conceptual Tie):** Reference the theme without quoting.
"I noticed you're focused on X..."

## AI Personalization Decision

**Generic AI company observations = 71% poor rate.** They perform 4x worse than no personalization.

What fails:
- "I saw your company helps customers with {{ai_summary}}..."
- "I noticed your company is doing great things in the [industry] space..."
- "As a leader in [AI-scraped industry]..."

What works (63.9% top-performer rate) — Targeted product-focused AI:
- "{{company_name}} could save {{savings}} annually by switching to [our approach]..."
- "For {{customer_type}} businesses like {{company_name}}, we typically see..."
- "Saw a case study about {{case_study_line}} — thought it might resonate..."

**The core rule:** AI should generate specific value props for them, not generic observations about them.

### When AI Context Works
- Targeting is broad enough that company context varies
- Your product's value changes based on what they do
- The variable adds genuine relevance, not filler

### When to Skip AI Context
- Narrow, homogeneous targeting (every prospect uses your product the same way)
- Commodity product with fixed use case
- The personalization would feel forced

## 5 Fallbacks When No Personalization Data
1. Demographic (buyer persona)
2. Firmographic (company segment)
3. Firmographic (industry vertical)
4. Firmographic (market geography)
5. Technographic (tech stack)

## AI Prompt Templates

**ICP identification:**
```
Find the top 3 most likely ICPs this person is prospecting based on {{companyDomain}}.
Rules: Job titles only (max 3), shorter abbreviations (CEO not Chief Executive Officer), always "and" before the last example, no full stop.
```

**Short company description:**
```
Describe the company in one concise sentence based on {{companyDomain}}.
Rules: Don't mention company name or ICP, maximum 8 words, neutral tone, all lower case.
Output fits: "Getting people interested in [insert] isn't the easiest thing in the world."
```

**Two-word subject line:**
```
Write a two-word email subject line based on {{companyDomain}}.
Rules: exactly 2 words, all lower case, relevant to their business, no sales/spam words.
```

**Top 3 problems:**
```
Identify top 3 problems of the main ICP based on {{companyDomain}}.
Requirements: directly linked to lagging indicators, rank 1-3, each max 10 words, no caps, no full stop, "and" before problem 3.
Format: [Problem 1], [Problem 2], and [Problem 3]
```

# Part 6 — QA & Benchmarks

## Pre-Send Checklist

Before sending, ask (Eric Nowoslawski):
1. How can I make this email worth this person's time?
2. How can I say it like a human would say it?
3. What can I say in this email that my competitors can't say?

Then confirm:
- [ ] First line is a specific observation/question/enriched data — not a generic AI summary and not "Hope you're doing well"
- [ ] At minimum `{{first_name}}` + `{{company_name}}` present
- [ ] Targeting a decision-maker (Owner/CEO/Founder/VP/Director), not an individual contributor
- [ ] Under ~70 words; every unnecessary word cut
- [ ] One direct-question CTA — no "If you're interested" or "Feel free to"
- [ ] A follow-up plan exists (value-add, not bump-only) including a breakup email
- [ ] No spam words or generic B2B filler (see Part 1)

## Top 10 Mistakes (Ranked by Impact)

| # | Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Generic AI first-line (71% poor rate) | Remove. Use targeted AI or specific enriched data. |
| 2 | No follow-up plan | Use 1–2 value-add follow-ups. Never just "bumping." |
| 3 | Bump-only follow-ups | Each follow-up needs new info: case study, different angle. |
| 4 | Generic greeting opener ("Hope you're doing well") | Open with specific observation, question, or enriched data. |
| 5 | Passive CTA ("If you're interested," "Feel free to") | End with a direct question. |
| 6 | No personalization at all | At minimum `{{first_name}}` + `{{company_name}}`. |
| 7 | Targeting individual contributors (<30% decision-makers) | Target Owner/CEO/Founder/VP/Director. |
| 8 | Scattered industry targeting (top industry <15%) | Focus on 1–2 industries with tailored copy. |
| 9 | Emails too long (300+ words) | Target ~70 words. Cut to essentials. |
| 10 | No breakup email | Add a final "last attempt" email — 10% top-performer rate. |

## Anti-Pattern Combos

- **Spray and Pray:** scattered targeting + no personalization + bump-only follow-ups → poor
- **Robot Writer:** AI whole email + no personalization + weak CTA → below average
- **AI Personalization Trap:** generic AI opener + basic variables → 71% poor rate
- **Enterprise Mistake:** enterprise target + long emails + too many sequences → poor
